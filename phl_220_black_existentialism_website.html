Philosophy & Hip-Hop Expression in Black Existentialism

Katie Dimick & Luke Frisinger | PHL 220 Final Project

This website explores how Black existentialist writers and artists explain identity, freedom, authenticity and struggle.

Introduction Black Existentialism Identity Struggle Hip-Hop Expression Discussion References

Introduction

Thesis: Black existentialist writers show that freedom alone is not enough to be authentic. Systems of racism and colonialism shape identities before an individual is able to define it for themselves, forcing an active struggle to create a self beyond societal predispositions.

What is existentialism?

Existentialism is a philosophy that focuses on freedom, choice and the meaning of life. It asks how people create identity in a world that does not always give clear answers. Existentialism often says that people must choose who they are, even when life is difficult or unfair.

What is authenticity?

Authenticity means living in a way that feels true to yourself. It is not just doing what society expects. In this project, authenticity means trying to define yourself even when racism, colonialism and social pressure try to define you first.

What is Black Existentialism?

Black existentialism looks at freedom, identity and meaning through the experience of Black life. It shows that identity is not created in a neutral world. Instead, racism and colonial history shape how Black people are seen and how they may come to see themselves.

Source 1

Frantz Fanon — Black Skin, White Masks

Frantz Fanon explains how racism affects the way Black people understand themselves. One of his most important ideas is the “white gaze.” This means that Black people are often forced to see themselves through the eyes of white society. Because of this, identity can become split. A person may know who they are internally, but society may treat them as something different.

Fanon shows that this split makes authenticity harder. The problem is not only personal. It comes from a racist world that shapes identity before a person has the chance to define themselves freely. This connects to the thesis because Fanon proves that freedom alone is not enough. A person must also fight against the outside forces that create false images of who they are.

Main idea: The “white gaze” shapes self-perception and creates a conflict between inner identity and outside judgment.

Source 2

Sylvia Wynter — Unsettling the Coloniality of Being

Sylvia Wynter argues that colonialism shaped the modern meaning of being human. According to Wynter, Western society created a narrow idea of humanity that centered whiteness and excluded Black people. This means Black identities were not simply misunderstood. They were pushed outside the main idea of what society called “human.”

Wynter helps explain Black existentialism because she shows that identity is connected to power. If society decides who counts as fully human, then people who are excluded must struggle to define themselves outside that system. Her work connects to the thesis because it shows that identity is shaped by colonial ideas before individuals can freely choose who they are.

Main idea: Colonial systems define identity and often devalue Black life.

Lived Experience of Identity Struggle

These sources show how the struggle for identity appears in real life and literature. They show that freedom is important, but it does not automatically create belonging, safety or authenticity.

Source 1

Richard Wright — The Outsider

Richard Wright’s The Outsider explores a character who has freedom but still feels separated from society. The story shows that freedom does not always lead to peace or belonging. Instead, it can lead to alienation when a person does not know where they fit in the world.

This source connects to the project because it shows that identity is not solved by simply being free. A person can be physically free while still feeling trapped by society’s expectations and racial meanings. Wright’s work supports the thesis because it shows that authenticity requires more than freedom. It requires the difficult work of creating a self in a world that already places labels on you.

Main idea: Freedom can still feel empty when society makes belonging difficult.

Source 2

Frederick Douglass — Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass Chapters 1, 6 and 10

Frederick Douglass’s narrative shows how slavery tried to destroy identity and selfhood. In Chapter 1, Douglass explains how enslaved people were separated from family history and basic knowledge about themselves. This shows how slavery controlled identity from the beginning.

In Chapter 6, Douglass learns that literacy is a path to freedom. Reading and writing help him understand the system of slavery and imagine a different life. In Chapter 10, Douglass’s resistance becomes a turning point. Fighting back helps him see himself as a person with power and dignity.

Douglass strongly supports the thesis because his life shows that authenticity is actively built through struggle. Freedom is not just given. It is created through learning, resistance and the refusal to accept the identity that slavery forced onto him.

Main idea: Literacy and resistance help Douglass build intellectual freedom and selfhood.

Source 3

Booker T. Washington — Up From Slavery Chapters 1, 5 and 14

Booker T. Washington’s Up From Slavery focuses on survival, discipline, education and gradual progress. In Chapter 1, he describes the difficult conditions of slavery and the lack of control enslaved people had over their lives. In Chapter 5, he emphasizes education and hard work as ways to move forward. In Chapter 14, he presents his belief that Black progress could happen through patience, labor and cooperation.

Washington’s view is important because it raises a harder question for the project: is adaptation the same as authenticity? Washington believed Black people could gain respect by proving themselves through work and discipline. However, this could also be seen as a limited form of freedom because it still works inside systems created by racism.

This source connects to the thesis by showing a different version of the struggle for selfhood. Washington shows survival and progress, but his work also makes us question whether authenticity is possible when choices are shaped by social limits.

Main idea: Washington values survival and gradual progress, but his work raises questions about constrained freedom.

Modern Expression of Black Existential Thought

Hip-hop can express philosophical questions in a modern form. It can show struggle, identity, pain, survival and resistance through music and storytelling.

Source 1

Kendrick Lamar — To Pimp a Butterfly

Kendrick Lamar’s album To Pimp a Butterfly expresses Black existential ideas through music. The album shows Kendrick struggling between who he is as a person and who the public expects him to be. He talks about fame, race, guilt, survival and systemic oppression.

The album also questions success. Kendrick shows that becoming famous does not automatically mean becoming free. Success can bring pressure, performance and new expectations. This connects to authenticity because Kendrick often asks whether he is being true to himself or acting out a role that society, fans or the music industry created for him.

This source connects strongly to the thesis because it shows Black existential struggle in the present. Like Fanon, Kendrick explores the conflict between inner self and outside image. Like Douglass, he shows that voice and expression can be tools of resistance. The album proves that Black existential thought is not only found in old books. It also lives in modern music.

Main idea: Kendrick Lamar uses hip-hop to explore identity, pressure, racism, authenticity and performance.
